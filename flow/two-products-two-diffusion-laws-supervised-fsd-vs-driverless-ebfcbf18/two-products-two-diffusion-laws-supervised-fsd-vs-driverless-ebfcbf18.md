### Recovered problem

Strip to bedrock: demand exists for cheaper rides globally, but incentives and liability attach to different products. Supervised FSD sells software to existing drivers under conventional tort; Robotaxi sells a transportation service where the OEM becomes the operator of record. Constraints and feedback loops diverge completely. The child imports the secondary constraint from Jurisdiction-Specific Driverless Commercial Authorization Is the Binding Constraint. The child imports the secondary constraint from Insurer Pull: Commercial Auto Capacity Blocks California Before Regulators Do.

### Hidden constraint

Demand: door-to-door mobility without owning a car. Incentive (OEM): FSD subscription scales with software margins and minimal new liability; Robotaxi captures trip revenue but concentrates crash, insurance, and regulatory capital costs. Constraint: driverless commercial permits (Texas yes, California deployment no). Feedback: each Texas incident tightens insurer and city geofences; each FSD mile with human supervisor improves models without triggering commercial AV rules. Global FSD rollout therefore cannot bootstrap driverless deployment velocity. It is tempered by Texas has an enforceable driverless commercial AV program—hence Austin, Dallas, Houston as the only unsupervised Robotaxi nodes. California lists Tesla Robotaxi LLC on testing-with-driver permits but not driverless testing or deployment, structurally blocking paid driverless service. FSD (Supervised) propagates globally because it remains human-supervised driving assistance under existing driver-licensing frames. Operational issues in Texas cities are secondary; they occur inside an already-authorized envelope. It is tempered by Invert stated problem: patchwork is not Tesla choosing Texas first but insurers refusing operator-of-record policies at scale in high-litigation states. When driverless service crosses a state line, commercial-auto terms change: higher premium, exclusion for teleop gaps, or capacity withdrawal. First market where insurance—not regulation—blocks paid driverless trips: California, where personal-auto FSD riders differ from commercial fleet policies and admitted-market capacity for driverless AV remains thin. Concrete event linking skip of California driverless deployment: premium quote or exclusion clause making per-mile economics negative despite testing-with-driver permit already held.
