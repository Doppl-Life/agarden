### Evaluation

#### Novelty +3

The permission-holder inversion and corridor-specific payout-swap/catastrophe-bond fusion is a fresh synthesis, but it largely recombines existing transition-finance and cat-bond templates rather than introducing a genuinely new mechanism.

#### Grounding +2

The institutional diagnosis—municipal fine yield, captive frequency margins, and treasury-gated rollout—tracks real AV politics, yet the swap triggers, basis-risk top-ups, and settlement spine lack corridor-level legal, actuarial, or fiscal baselines.

#### Falsifiability +3

First certified autonomous mile, >40% citation-revenue decline, and diverging fine-revenue versus rider-wait growth offer measurable gates, but survivor-fusion and repricing rules still leave key payout conditions underspecified.

#### Cost-efficiency -1

Per-corridor issuance of swaps, cat bonds, fleet hedges, and wear-charge capitalization implies heavy structuring and monitoring costs that may exceed simpler revenue-sharing or permit reforms unless scaled across many corridors.

#### Relevance +4

It squarely targets the case’s binding constraint—permission holders defending human-error revenue at the interconnection–treasury gate—by aligning their payoff with verified autonomous throughput rather than crash-frequency persistence.
