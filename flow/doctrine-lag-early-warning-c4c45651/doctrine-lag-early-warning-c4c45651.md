## Trace

### Case study · synopsis

A criminal team stages a political hostage crisis inside a sealed corporate facility to conceal a financial theft; the official response escalates through every standard protocol while the civilian insider — unknown to all parties — acts alone.

### Problem recovery · synopsis

The Insider Outside the Recognition Channel

## Discovery

### Finding 1

Rapid structural change leaves insider/outsider doctrines misaligned with who actually holds local knowledge. → field: [[insider-outsider-doctrine]]

### Finding 2

Insider-threat defense fails upstream at identification, before any detection pipeline can engage. → field: [[identification-bottleneck]]

### Finding 3

Threat-recognition models structurally exclude unintentional insiders whose risk is inaction, not adversarial intent. → field: [[unintentional-insider-gap]]

## Growth — Doppl

### Claim

Instrumenting the gap between who institutions recognize as inside and who demonstrably holds local knowledge turns structural misalignment into a measurable leading indicator—before insiders-outside-the-channel accumulate uncorrected influence debt.

### Implications

- Institutions that instrument doctrine lag gain reorganization and security lead time
- Actors exploiting recognition-channel blindness lose as the gap becomes visible
- Organizational sociology and threat-intelligence functions merge into a shared early-warning discipline

### Opportunities

- Doctrine-versus-ground-truth divergence indices for enterprises undergoing restructuring
- Cross-functional councils that refresh insider definitions on structural-change triggers
- Threat models that treat recognition-channel exclusion as an attack surface

### Evaluation

#### Cost-efficiency +0
