### Recovered problem

Strip the stop to bedrock: demand for collision reduction, demand for municipal cash flow, incentive to patrol high-yield corridors, constraint of due-process identity, feedback loop where citation volume funds more enforcement. AVs do not remove dangerous motion; they collapse only the revenue-feedback loop while leaving safety demand intact. ### Hidden constraint

Mechanism decomposition: safety demand → sensor/data substitutes; revenue demand → fine-revenue cliff with no automatic replacement; incentive → officers and cities routed toward high-citation geography; feedback → budget holes when AV share rises. Real cause is conflated demands treated as a single 'traffic enforcement' function. It is tempered by Invert the enforcement narrative: the stop exists to convert observable infraction into named payer. AV corridors remove the named payer while preserving the infraction signal, collapsing a revenue stream cities treated as incidental but budgeted around. Pilot corridors already show >40% fine-revenue drops — evidence that the stop was also a collection instrument. It is tempered by Invert the safety framing: map ledger categories (moving violations, equipment fixes, court assessments, forfeiture seizures) across home-rule cities and county sheriff contracts. Pilot corridors already show >40% fine-revenue drop; the first measurable break is when general-fund transfers from traffic courts miss bond-covenant floors (~$2–4M annual gap in mid-size pilots). Ordinary ticket decline is only wave one; wave two is the compliance substrate—warrant backlogs, uncollected court fees, idle impound yards—whose operating budget was ticket-volume-indexed. Fleet-account enforcement is the repricing move municipalities reach for once both waves land, not when citations merely dip.
