### Recovered problem

The stated rollout problem assumes regulators and consumers are the holdouts. The neglected stakeholder is everyone who never opted in—pedestrians, cyclists, other motorists, and municipal liability pools—who bear crash externalities while Tesla captures fare revenue and training data. Permission is blocked not by skepticism about capability but by the absence of a credible payer-of-last-resort for harms the fleet imposes on non-customers. ### Hidden constraint

Invert the adoption frame: instead of asking whether riders will trust Robotaxi, ask who underwrites injuries to parties with no contract, no app account, and no ability to opt out when a driverless Model Y enters shared road space. Texas commercial AV authorization and California's forced rebrand both tighten operator accountability, but neither resolves third-party compensation at fleet scale. Until a liability switch assigns default responsibility to the operator (not the human owner, not the victim, not fragmented insurers), cities treat geo-fenced pilots as tolerable experiments rather than fleet replacements. It is tempered by Tesla sold 'Full Self-Driving Capability' for years, then retroactively reframed contracts as supervised L2—creating a trust debt with early adopters while insulating the company from autonomous liability. A targeted flip for commercial dispatch only: when the Robotaxi app assigns a driverless fare, Tesla (or the licensed operator entity) assumes liability automatically; when the owner drives or supervises, legacy terms hold. Texas authorization becomes administratively satisfiable because the state can audit a single operator-of-record rather than millions of owners. Behavioral effect: regulators gain a clear enforcement hook; insurers gain a single insurable party; riders see responsibility aligned with marketing. It is tempered by Name the constraint before intervention: uneven permission is not geographic curiosity but a liability-regime seam. Inside the boundary (Austin, Dallas, Houston): Texas authorization, Robotaxi app, operator certificate, bystander recovery targets operator. Outside (California supervised FSD households): retroactive 'supervised' contracts keep owner as driver, blocking operator-subjection. Across the seam, experience diverges — Texas fleet composition shifts toward Tesla-owned Robotaxi; California fleet stays owner-supervised Level 2. Regulation diverges — Texas commercial authorization vs California DMV operator accountability. Arbitrage: deploy capital and insurance filings where operator subjecthood is recognized; harvest data and narrative from where it is not.
