### Claim

The neglected stakeholder is the insurance and liability layer, not displaced drivers. When attention stops being bundled into every trip, insurers must underwrite machine-hours instead of human fatigue—and they are structurally unprepared. The real bottleneck is who gets paid when vigilance is no longer a human job. The child imports the secondary constraint from Unbundle the Attention Price Floor. ### Implications

- Mechanism: Invert the displacement narrative: instead of optimizing cheaper rides, create an explicit 'attention reserve' market where fleet operators pre-purchase machine-supervised mile coverage from insurers and reinsurers. Regulators certify operating envelopes; insurers price residual risk per corridor and weather band. Uber/Lyft/Tesla compete on who can clear the lowest attention-adjusted premium, not just per-mile cost. Displaced driver income becomes a secondary political fight; the primary unlock is a liability product that makes unsupervised deployment insurable at scale. It is tempered by Regulators require fare decomposition: base mile + attention surcharge + autonomy discount. Supervised FSD trips carry a positive attention surcharge (funding attendant wages and training). Certified unsupervised trips apply a negative discount capped by proven ODD safety margin. Consumers see when they are buying machine miles vs. borrowed human vigil. Fleet operators compete to drive the attention surcharge to zero inside certified envelopes. Displaced-driver politics become legible: surcharge revenue phases into transition funds as discount widens. It is tempered by Commute floor: ATUS 2023 shows ~52 min round-trip daily vehicle travel for employed workers; at $28.50/hr median full-time wage the implicit attention price is ~$24.70/day or ~$6,400/yr per commuter. Long-haul floor: FMCSA 11-hour driving limit × $0.52/mile on 500-mile days with $28/hr implicit vigilance = ~$308/day attention cost embedded in freight rates. Demand releases only when Robotaxi trips substitute those minutes without re-imposing supervision attention. Falsifier: Texas passenger-seat attendant model keeps a $22/hr supervision floor on every trip, so cheaper rides do not release commuter attention—users still monitor the monitor. Second falsifier: modal switching to Robotaxi for parents adds pickup-wait attention (curbside vigilance for children) that offsets in-vehicle driving attention saved. The floor holds until ODD T3 clears and insurer telematics decouples.
- Claimed delta: Shifts the binding constraint from public job-loss optics to an underwritten risk market, unlocking legal deployment corridors before mass driver displacement becomes the headline. + Aligns demand signals with the true bottleneck—vigilance cost—so legal and operating-design progress directly shows up in consumer choice, accelerating adoption of genuinely driverless tiers. + Quantifies attention price floors for commute ($6,400/yr) and long-haul ($308/day) with two explicit falsifiers for when cheaper rides fail to release attention due to supervision or modal-switching overhead.

### Evaluation

#### Novelty +3

Reframing insurers as the primary stakeholder and inventing an attention-reserve market is a fresh synthesis, but liability as the AV deployment choke point is already a familiar industry argument.

#### Grounding +4

The commute and long-haul attention floors cite ATUS and FMCSA limits with wage assumptions, and the Texas attendant model ties the mechanism to a real supervised-deployment regulatory pattern.

#### Falsifiability +5

It names two concrete failure modes—persistent passenger-seat supervision and curbside pickup vigilance—and ties release of the attention floor to observable ODD-tier and telematics milestones.

#### Cost-efficiency +1

Strategically pivoting past driver-displacement politics may unlock deployment faster, but standing up reinsurer-backed reserve markets plus mandated fare decomposition is a heavy institutional build relative to simpler liability reforms.

#### Relevance +5

Under the case premise that legality, operating design, and adoption—not sensor performance—are the bottlenecks, it directly targets insurability and makes hidden vigilance costs legible in consumer pricing.
