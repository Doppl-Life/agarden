### Implications

- Mechanism: Binding constraint: ordinary mobility must pass through three separable legal events—(A) lien perfection via UCC-1 filing at title issuance, actor: captive lender or floorplan financier; (B) certificate-of-title transfer at each ownership change, actor: SOS/DMV and dealer; (C) registration renewal reattaching insurable interest, actor: insurer and state registry. Utilization economics alone cannot collapse these because a 90%-utilized fleet still fires A/B/C on every consumer acquisition. Trip accounts attack event B per trip by keeping title on the shell; event A fires once at fleet acquisition; event C fires annually on the shell, not per passenger. Falsifier: if high-utilization robotaxi fleets still show per-vehicle retail title transfers to owner-operators or per-trip insurance rebinds at scale, title continuity remains load-bearing and trip accounts have not replaced the collateral stack. Trip accounts win only when all three events decouple from trip frequency. It is tempered by Fleet shell holds one certificate of title and one perfected UCC-1 lien. Per ordinary trip, these events stop firing: (1) certificate-of-title transfer to passenger—eliminated; (2) UCC-1 partial release and re-perfection on trip collateral—eliminated; (3) lender notice-of-security-interest amendment per borrower—eliminated; (4) personal-auto policy bind keyed to passenger VIN—eliminated, replaced by fleet commercial policy; (5) retail lien perfection at point-of-sale title issuance—eliminated for trip consumers. Trip-account lever stays explicit: one small legal object (prepaid trip ledger + fleet shell) replaces the per-trip collateral stack. First observable break: UCC-1 partial release filing volume per 1,000 trip-miles falls below captive-auto-finance baseline while title-transfer latency for trip-class mobility approaches zero because no SOS application queue exists for passengers. This is not fleet-utilization forecasting—it is legal-event suppression measured in filing volume.
