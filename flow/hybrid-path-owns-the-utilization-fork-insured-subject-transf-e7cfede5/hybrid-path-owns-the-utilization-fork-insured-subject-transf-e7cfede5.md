### Recovered problem

The recovered problem treats hybrid ownership as primary—not universal unwind or pure-service replacement. Autonomy removes the human-driver rationale for idle household cars, but the binding question is whether privately financed vehicles cross a utilization threshold where they must earn fleet miles to justify captives, insurers, and trade-in math. Keep and shed are dependent branches; hybrid is the load-bearing fork until fleet service miles exceed roughly 35–40% of household transport demand in a metro. The child imports the secondary constraint from Insured Subject Transfers Before Title Transfers.

### Hidden constraint

Separate demand (household miles still wanted), incentives (captive finance and residual-value chains reward utilization over idle depreciation), constraints (financing terms assume owner-operated use; hybrid reprices the car as a yield asset), and feedback loops (rising fleet miles compress private residuals, which tightens hybrid loan terms and accelerates shed). Delete adoption-speed framing: unit sales can hold while the ownership premise breaks because the financed subject shifts from consumer durables to revenue inventory. It is tempered by Fuse insurance-subject transfer with ownership-fork logic: telematics plus OEM direct settlement pilots (TX, CA) disintermediate adjusters and reassign settlement authority to the OEM, which reprices household policies toward usage-based fleet riders. Threshold: when insurer and OEM rate filings assume fleet-operator liability above ~25% of autonomous miles in a state, household ownership is no longer the primary risk-bearing subject even if cars remain privately titled.

### Falsifier

Names hybrid as the primary path before dependents, supplies a measurable fleet-mile share threshold (~35–40% metro transport) at which household ownership becomes economically irrational even if nominal sales persist, and moves the clock from robotaxi hype to utilization economics. + Cites observable filing/permit changes shifting insured subject away from households, keeps the ownership-fork explicit, and adds a fleet-mile liability threshold where insurer assumptions imply hybrid—not household—primacy.
