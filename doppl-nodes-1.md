# When the Crashes Don't Come

## Context

As of June 22, 2026, this is a Tesla case. Waymo proves driverless ride-hailing can
operate, but its geofenced fleet model and prohibitive cost structure are not the
discontinuity being tested here.

The signals are no longer theoretical. Tesla's Robotaxi service is live in
limited areas of Austin, Dallas, and Houston. Texas now requires commercial
automated-vehicle operators to hold state authorization, turning a permissive
market into a regulated commercial channel. In California, Tesla Robotaxi LLC now
appears on the DMV testing-with-driver permit list; California has not granted
Tesla driverless deployment, but the barrier has moved into the regulatory
pipeline. Globally, Tesla says Full Self-Driving (Supervised) has already rolled
out in the United States, Canada, Mexico, Puerto Rico, Australia, New Zealand,
South Korea, the Netherlands, Lithuania, Estonia, Denmark, and Belgium.
China is a messier but important signal: Tesla announced FSD Supervised
availability there in May 2026, while full-market regulatory clearance and
fleet-wide availability remain less clean.

The premise of this case is that Tesla has solved full self-driving. The question is what happens if the bottleneck is no longer invention, but permission and adoption, across an existing vehicle platform.

Suppose Tesla's model starts to produce the result everyone wanted: fewer crashes caused by human driving error en masse, without rebuilding the fleet city by city. The safety story is not ambiguous. Fewer people are killed, fewer people are injured, fewer families get the phone call no one wants.

The public conversation does not get very far. The visible story is that rides
get much cheaper and taxi, Uber, and Lyft drivers lose work. Investors talk about
ride-hailing economics. Labor reporters talk about drivers. Local officials talk
about street use, airport pickups, and whether the service is allowed. For most
people, that is the whole frame.

Almost nobody treats crash reduction as an economic demand shock. A crash is not only a bad event; it is also the beginning of a long chain of paid work, supply lines, and public cost. Someone files a claim. Someone tows the vehicle. Someone repairs, stores, salvages, auctions, litigates, bills, treats, rehabilitates, or administers the consequences. Some organizations are paid per incident. Others maintain capacity because severe crashes arrive often enough to justify it.

No one is trying to preserve crashes. That is not the question. The question is
what has to adapt when a massive recurring source of harm falls away, especially
if most of the exposed systems are not yet thinking of themselves as exposed.

## The Situation

The rollout is uneven and the architecture matters. A software-driven fleet that
can be enabled across many existing vehicles changes the timing, geography, and
scale of the shock. Texas shows the public-service side of the Tesla rollout.
California shows the permission boundary: testing with a driver is not driverless
deployment. The larger question is what happens if permission turns a working
autonomy stack into broad deployment before adjacent institutions have named the
risk.

Liability may also shift: a remaining crash might involve a fleet operator,
manufacturer, software provider, sensor supplier, owner, passenger, or road
authority rather than a single human driver.

The remaining crashes may not look like today's average crashes either. There may be
fewer of them, but some individual incidents could be more expensive because modern
vehicles carry sensors, calibration requirements, software records, and specialized
parts.

The obvious labor story is only one surface. A lower-crash world may also touch:

- insurers, agents, reinsurers, adjusters, and claims organizations
- collision repair, parts, towing, storage, salvage, and auction businesses
- injury lawyers, courts, settlement services, and billing intermediaries
- emergency medical services, trauma centers, rehabilitation providers, and
disability systems
- public agencies and households that absorb crash costs, lost work, disruption,
and administrative burden

Traffic stops, fines, forfeiture, driver licensing, and license-as-ID may also
change under autonomy, but they are not the center of this case unless the link runs
through crash harm.

## Decision Point

You are looking past the visible cheap-rides-and-driver-displacement story.

What should the dependency map include, and which crash-linked businesses,
public agencies, medical systems, legal systems, and household costs need the
closest attention if human-caused crash volume falls sharply?

## Synopsis

With Tesla robotaxi service live in parts of Texas, the case examines what happens to the large economy built around vehicle crashes as autonomy makes them rarer.
